Comparative Study On Different Types Of Motherboards Computer Motherboard
A motherboard is a hardware component of the computer; it is the main circuit board that holds the necessary and essential processing parts of the computer. Every part of the component provides for a purpose, either to communicate with another component or to receive power. The motherboard's components are classified in 4 parts which are the chipset, CPU socket, expansion slot, and memory slot (Sabri et al., 2018).
Discussion What is the main purpose of the motherboard?
According to a study by Tolmjanovic et al. (2013), the motherboard is the essential core component of a computer and is often overlooked compared to other hardware parts. The main purpose is to connect the important parts that power the computer, such as the memory, central processing unit (CPU) , disks, cards, power supply, and other components, for a convenient and quick way. The motherboard is located inside the computer case and is attached by screws through holes that are already drilled. The motherboard has only one slot for the CPU, while in the memory it has 2 or 4, depending on what type of motherboard or brand. In the hard drives or solid-state drives, most motherboards include one connector only, but sometimes they include multiple connectors for it. It also includes slots for various cards, especially for graphics cards, audio cards, etc. The power from the power supply is provided to the motherboard via a specially designed connector. The I/O panel (Input/Output) or back panel connectors contain all the external connections, mostly for peripherals, such as USB, Ethernet audio, monitor, mouse, keyboard, and more.
Parts of the motherboard CHIPSET
Are group of integrated circuits on the motherboard that controls on how the computer hardware and buses interact with the CPU and other components.
MEMORY SLOT
This is where you place you RAM sticks in the motherboard.
PCI/PCIE SLOT
This is where you connect your expansion cards or components to be added to the computer.
SATA
This is where you connect your storage devices like HDD or SSD to the motherboard.
Legacy Bios and UEFI BIOS
BIOS (Basic Input/Output System) is a integrated software for your motherboard which its main purpose is for booting up your system. This provides important instructions to your hardware and checks if it’s functioning properly (Rowley, 2024).
LEGACY BIOS
This is a older version of the software that is not widely used and available in today's modern hardware and compatible with old operating systems (Rowley, 2024).
UEFI
UEFI (Unified Extensible Firmware Interface) is latest version of legacy BIOS. They are quite similar but UEFI provides a notable improvements that boasts the hardware, compatibility for latest-gen hardware, better performance, more secure, and improved interface (Rowley, 2024).
Types of Motherboards 1. AT (Advanced Technical) MOTHERBOARD
AT motherboards were developed back then in the 1980s and it stands for advanced technical. This motherboard was specific in size and shape, it only fits in AT computer cases, and has limited connectors and ports in comparison to today's motherboard. You can find in the AT motherboard a huge power connector and several slots for extra parts along the bottom edge. The processor is connected to the top right corner and memory slots are near to the processor. It used a system called ISA to add parts to the computer.
2. ATX (Advanced Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
ATX motherboard is the most common motherboard found in desktop computers globally. Its popularity stems from its efficient performance and versatility. This motherboard contains numerous I/O ports, PCIe lanes, and SATA connections that cater well for a computer system. Since 2017, this motherboard has become the norm for many personal computer systems builds.
3. BTX (Balanced Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
BTX motherboard (Balanced Technology eXtended) is a form factor introduced by intel in 2003 as a replacement for ATX. It features a low profile, more efficient layout to better cooling facilitation, a scalable compact board to accommodate in different system sizes, and to support large mass motherboard components.
4. EATX (Extended Advanced Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
Extended ATX motherboard (EATX) can support both mainstream desktop and but mostly used in workstation processors. It is slightly bigger than an ATX motherboard that contains more PCIe connectors, especially for workstation variants. Sometimes they support dual-socket support, allowing you to run two CPUs in the same board, the configuration is old now, and in today's there are not many newer examples with the current generations. It has multiple memory DIMM slots than the most common motherboards have. These are expensive to buy and to operate, because they are designed to facilitate most of the power and performance possible being cooling is a big part.
5. LPX (Low Profile eXtension) MOTHERBOARD
LPX motherboard (Low Profile eXtension) is a motherboard developed in 1987. This motherboard uses a riser card, and has different placement of the parallel, serial, and PS/2 ports compared to other motherboards. LPX is designed for lower compact computer cases and makes it ideal for office environments or home set-ups.
6. MICRO-ATX (Micro Advanced Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
Micro-ATX is slightly larger than mini-ITX that is used in slightly larger computer systems. It features multiple expansion slots, mostly containing 4 memory slots, and a variety of ports and connectors fewer than an ATX for different peripherals. This motherboard provides great flexibility and upgradability, and accommodates larger components such as full-sized graphics cards.
7. MINI-ITX (Information Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
Mini-ITX (Information Technology eXtended) is a small compact and versatile motherboard form factor that is found in small form factor (SFF) computers. It is roughly two-thirds the size of a standard ATX motherboard. It is designed for energy efficiency and mostly boasts low power consumption. It still contains the expansion slot, memory slot, ports, connectors, and PCIe slots but is very limited due to its size.
8. PICO BTX (Balanced Technology eXtended) MOTHERBOARD
Pico BTX motherboard is a smaller version of a BTX motherboard. This motherboard only contains 2 expansion slots that are being supported. It provides half-height cards or riser cards features which can be helpful for demands of the application that are digital.
